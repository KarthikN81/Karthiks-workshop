Income tax — England, Wales & NI

Personal Allowance£0 – £12,5700%

Personal Allowance tapers by £1 for every £2 of income over £100,000 and reaches £0 at £125,140 — creating an effective 60% marginal rate between £100,000–£125,140. These bands and the Personal Allowance apply separately to each partner — state pension, pension drawdown and any other income are added together to find each person's own band individually; ISA income is never included for either partner.

Capital Gains Tax (general investment accounts only)

TaxpayerRate on gains
Within basic rate band18%
Higher / additional rate24%
Annual exempt amount: £3,000 per person, use-it-or-lose-it. The UK does not distinguish short-term vs long-term holding periods for CGT — rate depends only on your total income plus the gain, not how long you held the asset. CGT is irrelevant inside an ISA or pension: both wrappers are entirely CGT-free, which is why this plan never generates a CGT figure for those buckets. The "other taxable investments" bucket is the exception — withdrawals there would typically trigger CGT on any gain, not income tax; this model simplifies by treating it as income tax for consistency, so treat its tax figures as indicative only.

ISA withdrawals are always 100% tax-free with no reporting needed. Pension withdrawals beyond the tax-free cash are taxed as ordinary income, stacked on top of your state pension. Taking any taxable pension income cuts your future pension contribution allowance to £10,000/yr (MPAA) — rarely relevant once fully retired, but worth knowing if you might return to work.

The full state pension alone uses almost all of one Personal Allowance, leaving very little headroom for tax-free pension drawdown for that partner — which is exactly why the ISA and tax-free cash buckets matter most once it starts. State pension is taxable income but paid gross with no tax deducted at source; any tax due on it is collected via PAYE on another pension or via Self Assessment, calculated separately for each partner.

Why a couple's numbers aren't double a single person's

One person's Personal Allowance£12,570
Two partners' combined allowance£25,140
One person's basic-rate ceiling£50,270
Two partners' combined basic-rate ceiling£100,540
Tax is calculated separately for each partner in this model, not on the household's combined income. A couple drawing £25,140 between them — split evenly — pays no income tax at all, where a single person drawing the same total would pay tax on everything above their one £12,570 allowance. This is the single biggest lever available to a couple: splitting withdrawals so both partners use their own allowance and basic-rate band each year, rather than funnelling income through just one of them.
How the household model works: the combined annual spending need is split evenly between both partners every year, and each partner then runs the selected strategy independently across their own four buckets — using their own age, their own state pension timing, and crucially their own Personal Allowance. Income tax is calculated separately for each partner and then added together; the model never pools income before taxing it, since UK income tax doesn't work that way for a married couple or civil partnership (each person is taxed individually; there's no UK joint filing). If one partner's own buckets run dry before the other's — a common outcome when balances are uneven — the partner with spare capacity automatically covers the shortfall using their own buckets and own strategy, rather than the household being treated as "out of money" just because that year's even split happened to be uneven against their respective balances. The household is only shown as depleted once both partners are genuinely unable to find any more money between them.

How the strategies work: two rules apply in every strategy, for each partner individually, overriding its normal order where relevant. First, the "other taxable investments" bucket is held back as a last resort for that partner — only drawn once their own ISA, pension tax-free cash, and taxable pension balance are all fully depleted. Second, any year that would otherwise land at £0 tax for that partner is automatically topped up so that at least their own full Personal Allowance (£12,570) is drawn from their state pension plus taxable pension combined — money is swapped out of their ISA or tax-free cash and into taxable pension instead, so neither partner's allowance is left unused. This never increases what either partner withdraws overall, and never pushes a genuinely zero-tax year into owing tax. Within those two rules: the tax-minimising strategy draws state pension first, then tax-free cash, then fills the remaining Personal Allowance from taxable pension, then tops up from the ISA. The "taxable pension to Personal Allowance, then split evenly" strategy fills the Personal Allowance from taxable pension first, then splits anything further equally between ISA and tax-free cash. MyIdea fills the Personal Allowance first, then drains tax-free cash completely, then the ISA completely, before falling back to further taxable pension. All strategies use each bucket's own growth rate as set per partner in the sidebar, and ignore investment volatility, fees, platform charges, and means-tested benefit interactions.

This is a planning illustration, not financial or tax advice. It uses 2026/27 rates and thresholds held constant for the whole projection — in reality, allowances, thresholds and pension rules change frequently (the Personal Allowance freeze alone is currently legislated only to April 2031). It also doesn't account for the Marriage Allowance (transferring up to £1,260 of unused Personal Allowance between spouses/civil partners, available when one partner earns less than the Personal Allowance and the other is a basic-rate taxpayer) or inheritance tax planning between partners, both of which can matter for a couple's overall position. Each partner's own pension scheme rules, marginal tax position, National Insurance record, and any Scottish tax bands may differ from what's modelled here.

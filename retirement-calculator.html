About the numbers. The straight-line model compounds your balance at a single assumed rate every year — it shows what happens if returns were perfectly smooth. The Monte Carlo model instead draws from 100 actual calendar-year S&P 500 total returns (1926–2025, dividends reinvested, source: S&P 500 historical return series) to build thousands of possible futures, so it captures the bad years along with the good. Both models adjust spending for your inflation assumption and report results in today's purchasing power.

Why Monte Carlo numbers often run higher. A single "expected CAGR" like 7% is usually a deliberately conservative planning assumption. The actual 1926–2025 record compounded faster than that — so simulations built from real history can show larger and more volatile outcomes than the straight-line model, in both directions. Lower your growth assumption or shorten the historical window if you want the two methods to align more closely.

This tool is for education and planning intuition only — it is not financial advice, and past market returns do not guarantee future results.
